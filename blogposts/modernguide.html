Introduction


This is my second post and that may be a disclaimer… I’m really not sure yet. Also, this won’t be a step-by-step guide.

“Two disclaimers already?!”

Without hesitation, let me introduce myself. I’m Frankie Valentine. I currently am a freelance web developer and designer. I previously held a full-time technical sourcing role for a couple startups and a large corporate companies in the San Francisco Bay Area. I’m one month away from turning 29 and a year into learning web development I am, well, admittedly still quite new at this. I didn’t graduate with a CS degree, and yes, I firmly believe you don’t need a CS degree to be a successful developer.

I have always been interested in technology. I still am. I am fond of minimalistic design, creating things that will be easy use, and always learning new material. That last one. If you want to be a successful developer I can’t stress how important it is to have a passion to always be learning new material. More on that later…

So why are we here?

Funny you should ask…

Since I began my journey on learning web development I have been bookmarking and logging my progress. I’ve been on a mission to get a guide put together for those looking to begin their own journeys. I have taken the steps, the tutorials, found the hidden gems, the diamonds in the rough, and here I present them to you.

I reflected to starting out as a noob looking high and low for resources on how to get started. My process was flawed at the most, knowing what I do now, but without acknowledging or embracing our flaws where would we be? How would we turn out without our failures? Failures turn into successes.

I created this to be a _modern_ day resource. I created this to display a particular order in which I believe would have been beneficial when I started. This guide is a light in the dark for those aspiring developer’s who need it the most.

What we will cover.

This guide will begin by covering helpful tools, tips, and resources toward becoming a self-taught web developer. I will go over what to consider before choosing this as a career as well as why it’s a great choice. This guide will cover terms to know as a beginner, getting set up with social media and what accounts are important to have before starting. It will go into what to learn, when to learn it, and the resources for learning it.

Tools are an important aspect of being a developer. Things others have created to make processes faster and more efficient are wonderful for new web developers. I will cover the tools that I have come across that have made life incredibly easier as a modern day web developer.

Lastly, it will cover freelancing, project management, and what to know before the big interview if that is the route you choose.

A final note

This is not the first guide and the only guide. It shouldn’t be taken as the comprehensive outline above all others available to becoming and learning web development. There are a ton of resources out there that may not be listed in this guide. I have encountered countless valuable articles, blog posts, handbooks, and guides that accomplish more, if not, most of what I go over in this guide. I want that to be known before you dive in.

Credit should be directed to this wonderful community and those that have created those valuable resources outlined in this guide.

Before you consider web development

Web Development is a track for those that can grind, research, and be persistent about not giving up on a problem. Before considering development I believe that must be taken into account. You should know if your skill sets align so that you can grasp the high level concepts ahead of you.

I do not wish to have my opinions turn you from the career. Just to have this be introduced as a forewarning is my mission. These skills can be gained over time and they will be important to your success.

This career is still excelling immensely. There is plenty of opportunity available. The endless supply of jobs will fuel you to move forward. The items I list in this guide will help advance you quickly and the tools that are available to learn quickly are more accessible than ever.

Maybe you are at a cross between which part of the spectrum you will fit in. In my case, for instance, I really love to code and piece things together while also keeping a keen eye for design. If this sounds like you too, then you may want to follow the full-stack designer path. You could just determine you like to develop what the user will see on the page specifically and not how its setup behind the scenes. That would be a path leading to front-end development. It’s important to know the differences. You don’t have to learn everything in this guide. The work you are passionate about will determine your career path.

Secrets for being self-taught

Everyone wants the secret(s) to being a self-taught developer. In my opinion, they are pretty straightforward, and relatable to any other career you will pursue. Personally, I feel to have been very lucky to be able to have the time, accessibility, routine, and confidence to move forward with web development as a career.

Time is key. Learning web development will take A LOT of time. If you aren’t serious about having consistent blocks laid out in your routine it will be a long, dense, and dreadful route. Which, of course, management of that time is also a huge part of learning your craft. If you don’t have the accountability to create schedules for yourself, to-do lists, and maximize your project management skills I believe you will struggle.

Accessibility will establish you becoming a fearless developer. If you don’t have access to the tools necessary to learn your craft, it will hinder your performance. Every developer should be accessible and flexible, in my opinion. Being able to look at documentation, know how to use it, have it accessible locally, and be able to pick it up at a moments notice is what employers look for. Not to mention help your workflow tremendously.

As soon as you are up and running establish as much of a routine as you can. I highly suggest the hour of code a day. Whether that be taking in as many lessons as you can within the hour, diving into documentation, becoming an expert at the new tool you downloaded, and or getting a start on that new component you want to build out. This can be as simple as laying out the groundwork for your HTML document and then applying the CSS the next day.

#100daysofcode will get you in the swing of generating a routine. Stay committed.

GitHub - Kallaway/100-days-of-code: Fork this template for the 100 days journal - to keep yourself accountable

Confidence is key. Bet you haven’t heard that before. I believe that you must learn the foundations before becoming confident. Don’t stray away from your learning map with concepts you aren’t ready to grasp yet. Cutting corners in this process will only hinder your advancement. Crawl before you can walk.

Accounts setup

Upon gaining recognition of the terms you will need to know moving forward we’ll get setup presenting yourself and your work online. These accounts will be utilized daily.

Some may already have accounts in the following, but when I began I did not. I will reference the sites and give a brief blurb into what they about if you were not aware like I was.

  • Stackoverflow

    • Large, online community for developers to learn, and share programming knowledge.

  • Quora

    • Similar to StackOverflow. You can ask and share knowledge by the community you wish to follow. In our case web development.

  • Reddit

    • A discussion site, filled with social news, sub-postings (subreddits), content rating, and ask me anything (AMA) interviews from people of all walks of life.

  • Github

    • A hosting service for version control using Git.

  • CodePen

    • A social development environment for front-end designers and developers. Show off your work here, test your code, and see it live.

  • Glitch

    • Similar to CodePen, you can host your project live here.

  • Medium

    • A place to read, write, and interact with stories from developers. You will learn a lot from plenty of talented individuals in the community, some I have already linked to in fact.

  • Meetup

    • An online social networking service for people to organize a meet or join live group meetings.

  • Trello

    • A project management tool that I use daily to track progress in projects I am working on.

  • Heroku

    • A cloud platform as a service that enables developers to build, run, and operate their applications.

  • Google

    • I listed Google for its use of the Google Analytics, Cloud Platform, Domains, URL shortener, and API’s. You will need a Google account to utilize those. ## Get setup for development
